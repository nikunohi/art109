The Future Web

On a cloudy June morning in DC nearly two decades ago, United States Senator Ted Stevens of Alaska stood proudly before his committee and declared his distaste for an internet he deemed too… tubular?

I’ll leave the quote below so you can get a better idea of what he was going for…

“An [email] was sent by my staff at 10 o'clock in the morning on Friday. I got it yesterday [Tuesday]. Why? Because it got tangled up with all these things going on the Internet commercially. [...]The Internet is not something that you just dump something on. It's not a big truck. It's a series of tubes. And if you don't understand, those tubes can be filled and if they are filled, when you put your message in, it gets in line and it's going to be delayed by anyone that puts into that tube enormous amounts of material.”

I don’t fully blame him for his childlike understanding of the internet. It’s common to assign things we don’t completely understand magical, sometimes even tubular, properties. Besides, we’re all human. Even at 83 years old at the time of this convoluted metaphor, Senator Stevens still had some learning to do about this emergent phenomenon that would only grow more and more prominent.

I will also admit that I am in a similar boat as Senator Stevens. While I’ve used the internet for as long as I can remember, and am way passed a tube-based understanding of it, I’ve only recently started actively creating things for the internet, and have much to learn about the technical intricacies of the web we take for granted. However, I feel like my relationship with the internet is a bit more conducive to its continued well-being.

This is to say, the problem with Ted’s comments is not in the absurdly anachronistic metaphor he employed, but that his only understanding of the internet is within the context of its use as a tool or technology.

In present, I’m sad to report that things have only worsened during the time since the senator’s speech. While social media has revolutionized the way we communicate through the internet, and many large companies have taken over the search results with their homepages, our consumption of the internet is headed towards the extremes of needs-based use. With the inception of LLMs and consumer-accessible AI, this trouble furthers as we resort to and rely on the internet rather than more mindfully integrating it into our everyday lives. It seems like many of the people who use the internet nowadays only do so out of a growing need, at some point crossing the border to dependency.

While Ted’s imagination only took him as far as a truck or a tube, I hope that, in the future, the internet turns out to be more like a lively street corner....

. . .

...A hundred years from now, the internet will be a street corner frequented by people who care about the well-being of the internet outside of its consideration for the “most convenient human innovation” awards.

Sure, there will be many conveniences to be enjoyed from visiting the street corner—what with the camaraderie and business conducted there—but people will neither exclusively live on the corner for its sheer ease of use, nor will they only begrudgingly swing by when an issue is large enough to trump their apprehension to and distrust of the corner.

Most importantly, people will begin to see the internet as a place in time rather than a tool or technology. It is because of our presence on the internet at this current time in culture and society—rather than the mere existence of the internet itself—that we are afforded opportunities to nurture relationships, partake in culture, or learn new truths. Because the corner is so ubiquitous yet digestible, accessible yet well-maintained, we are drawn to it—as a place adjacent, not distinct, to the physical world—to build recreation, identity, and business, among countless other things.

In this place in time, the internet will truly be for those to explore. Rather than the current craze around hyper-curated feeds, we will have to actively seek out and form our sensibilities surrounding the internet and what it offers. Freshly dropped off near the entrance of this vast community, you could easily stumble upon a seemingly mundane website archiving poorly parked cars in a certain area, or perhaps a web-based recipe book put together by an anonymous grandparent, or maybe a massive online marketplace. No matter what may be out there, only through venturing out and truly experiencing this new net world will we grow to understand the culture and norms of the new digital street corner. These experiences will also give us a more meaningful relationship and understanding of the net as a whole, and invest us more heavily in its maintenance and care.

In reality, the ideal net of the future won’t be so dissimilar from the net of old; a vast street corner whose countless denizens are unfettered by curation and undeterred from settling their own plot on the avenue, populating it with anything they please. In the not-so-distant past, we had an internet which, if not nearly as robust, was almost completely made up of and supported by those who understood not only its technological potential, but also its enormous social and cultural potential. As a result of their optimism and wide-eyed wonder, these netizens had a more meaningful relationship with the internet that kept them invested in the beautification of their digital space. And people... just kinda made what they wanted to and put it on the web. "Beautification" wasn't an exercise in making their sites as visible or as navigable as possible; it was a reflection of the sensibilities they had nurtured from their experiences at the street corner. These same sensibilities can't be developed in an environment where everything around us eventually terminates into what gets us the most clicks, or keeps us on top of the latest trends.

So I guess what I really see in the ideal future of the web is more sincerity; for those who visit the street corner to treat it with as much wonder and genuine curiosity as those who remember a time when the web wasn't just seen as a means to an end.

. . .

Ironically enough, maybe my ideal future internet reflects a more earnest and culturally vibrant ecosystem that we lost not so long before a certain senator shared his speculations.

Miss you at least a little bit, Ted.
